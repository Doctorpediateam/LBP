Tips for Managing Lower Back Pain

What treatments are available for lower back pain? Many can be adapted for each individual's needs and include anything from over-the-counter medications to low-impact aerobics. Depending on your specific diagnosis, you may find one better suited to you than it might be to others – so try what you can and see what works best for you. You may even try a combination of these treatments. Before starting any of them, however, check with a doctor to make sure the treatment won’t aggravate your condition.

Self Care

Fortunately, there are a number of things you can do without a physician's assistance that can help with back pain.

Small Rest Breaks – On the one hand, you don't want to spend too much time laying/sitting down as too much inactivity is harmful to the healing process. But staying away from strenuous activities for a day or two is recommended.

Activities – Make sure that you're not resting in once place for too long. If, for example, you're sitting down for long stretches of time, get up around every 20 minutes or so for a brief walk/stretch. If standing hurts, try avoiding doing things that require you to stand for long periods at a time.

Ice/Heat – Heat, be it from a bath, heating pad, heat wrap or hot water bottle, tends to relax tense muscles and increase the flow of blood. Why is that good? Heightened blood flow delivers both oxygen and nutrients that the muscles require to heal correctly. If your problem is caused by inflammation, then turn to ice packs to bring the swelling down. You can also cross between ice and heat – but make sure you protect your skin before applying the heat and ice to it!

Over The Counter Meds – Some familiar, easy-to-find medications will help with pain. They include aspirin, naproxen, acetaminophen, and ibuprofen. Acetaminophen (Tylenol, for example) stops the pain signals that nerves carry to the brain. The other three forms of drugs (including Bayer, Advil, and Aleve) are anti-inflammatory so they help with swollen nerves or muscles.

You don't need a doctor to follow any of these treatments, but still, you should use them with care and attention. If you're not sure what's best for you, by all means, consult your physician.

Exercises

Again, as long as you don't over-do anything, you can find benefits from stretching muscles in the lower back. You can also find them in strengthening exercises (the McKenzie Method and Dynamic Lumbar Stabilization are two that are recommended to help with lower back pain) and low-impact aerobics such as swimming, walking, and stationary bikes.

Raising your heart rate by any form of physical activity for extended periods of time might help. Often, spinal structures become stiff, uncomfortable and sometimes worse if they're unused for long periods.
