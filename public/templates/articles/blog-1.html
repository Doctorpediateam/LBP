5 Tips for Getting a Diagnosis for Lower Back Pain

If you're suffering from lower back pain, how do you get a solid diagnosis?

You might be surprised that obtaining an accurate diagnosis is harder than it sounds – and that's a result of the sometimes complex and multi-issue causes of back pain in the first place. Certain diagnoses are going to be fairly straightforward (including fractures, infections or even tumors) but there might be trouble getting spine specialists to agree on one for you depending on the causes.

The Process

To get a medical (or clinical) diagnosis, a doctor needs to better understand the cause. So, in order to obtain one, there are up to three steps they will take.

Comprehensive Review

A doctor first needs to find out the patient's medical history so to that end they will ask questions regarding when the pain occurs, how it feels when present, and what positions/activities affect the level of pain.

Exam

A  health care provider conducts a physical exam testing for, amongst other things, nerve function and muscle strength. The physical exam gives the physician a much clearer idea of what type of back issues the patient may have.

Diagnostic

If called for, a third step would include a diagnostic test (they include CT and/or MRI scans) that looks for a something that might explain the back pain. If, say, a disc is thought to be the cause, a test can be ordered to determine the location and size of it as well as a look at the nerves in the area. Other diagnostic tests include (but are not limited to) x-rays.

Language Issues

Different doctors use different terminology to describe the same medical conditions so be sure to get clarifications on whatever your doctor tells you. For example, the same condition might be referred to as a herniated disc, or a pinched nerve, or a variety of different "discs" – including bulging, protruding, slipped and prolapsed disc.

Other times, language can be out-dated or simply confusing. For example, the term "degenerative disc disease" is misleading because it's not actually a disease – but rather a condition.

What Do Diagnostic Tests Do?

They don't tell the entire story, that's for sure – but what they do is provide essential clues to the bigger picture. So a doctor will use the results from a diagnostic test in tandem with his/her physical exam along with your personal symptoms. A diagnostic is mostly there to verify anatomical lesions as the reason for discomfort/pain.

Be Aware!

Certain forms of diagnostic tests are not yet embraced as best practices by the entirety of the medical community.  Some controversial methods include  EMG, selective nerve root blocking and facet blocking as the efficacy of their results is not yet determined. Another controversial method is a discogram – a means of injection to determine which discs might be responsible for the pain.

No Reasons Found

If your doctor is unable to discover a particular reason for the cause of your back pain, there are still treatments available. For example, pain medicine specialists look into methods of relieving pain that surgery can't solve. You can also join support groups or get counseling from reputable pain clinics.
